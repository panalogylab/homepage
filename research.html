At Panalogy, we work on designing interdisciplinary frameworks to understand and analyze emergent behaviors of social AI systems. With insights gained from this understanding, we hope to make valuable contributions to developments in alignment research while improving quality of social AI systems operating in the real world.

Overview

We perceive Social AI as an emerging field that amalgamates insights and principles from diverse domains to develop long-lived AI agents capable of interacting with humans and other computational artifacts naturally and intelligently in intricate social environments. These agents can discern social cues, comprehend human emotions, and react in a manner that is both socially appropriate and engaging. They engage with humans in a way that feels natural and intuitive, facilitating communication and augmenting human-AI social interactions and understanding.

One crucial aspect of social AI, when the agents start operating in real social environments, is set of emergent behaviours of those environments that will underpin the dynamics of the agents' alignment with human values and intentions. We believe that a solid scientific understanding of those behaviours has two far-reaching implications for successful design and deployment of social AI having desirable alignment properties.

The first implication is that such an understanding can be used to develop powerful feedback mechanisms that help align the agents' behaviours with desirable social values. Without these mechanisms or the capability to utilize them effectively, a state-of-the-art social AI product may fail hard and fast in complex social context where unexpected behaviours emerge without any effective feedback handling and evaluation.

The second implication is that being able to scientifically study social AI’s emergent behaviours provide us with a concrete ground for analysing their social and economic impacts on our societies. What is becoming more apparent with the recent development of emergent cognitive capabilities (those not foreseeable at the creation of such AI), is the field of social AI also encompasses the study of the social and economic implications of the rapid advances in artificial intelligence and distributed computing technologies. These recent advances have essentially created complex and dynamic social and economic entanglements that are difficult to analyse and understand using conventional methods and ways of thinking.

In this context, social AI research also involves developing new theories and practices that can capture the interactions between humans and intelligent machines in complex social systems. This may involve studying the impact of AI on employment, social inequality, privacy, and other social and economic factors, as well as exploring new approaches to designing and regulating these systems in a way that maximises their benefits while minimising their risks.

At Panalogy Lab, we focus on designing social AI based on concrete scientific understanding of their emergent social behaviours, hoping to create innovative solutions that break down disciplinary boundaries and explore the interconnectedness of all things. Our commitment to lifelong learning, scientific rigour and sound empirical research allows us to deliver solutions that are based on an interdisplinary understanding of behavioural complexity and uncertainties in real-world social settings.

We believe that social AI has the potential to transform the way we live, work, and interact with the world around us. we are proud to be leading the way in social AI research and design, and are committed to creating new value for research communities and policy makers as we continue to explore the possibilities of social AI and its transformative potential.


Social AI Alignment

We are at the beginning of an age where AI systems operate as long-lived agents interacting with humans and other artifacts in real-world complex social environments. Aligning these social AI systems, when they are operating in societies, with evolving human intentions and values is a critical challenge whose complexity we still know little about. At Panalogy Lab, we take an approach to alignment research built upon an interdisciplinary understanding of emergent behaviors of social AI systems within the complex social context in which they operate. Our research focuses on three main themes:

1. Complex decision-making capability: We study behaviors of AI systems that exhibits capability to make complex social decisions. Currently, our focus of attention is on studying augmented language models using a mixture of prompt engineering and reinforcement learning

2. Behavioral complexity: We seek to study behavioral complexity of AI systems in evolving complex social context rather than rigidly managed and simplified environments. Currently, we are investigating social settings that are pivotal in human societies.

3. Training AI systems to do research: Our long-term goal is to be able to train AI systems that can effectively do complex social alignment research using our own insights into behavioral complexity. We pursue this approach because we believe social AI systems will soon operate in environments whose complexity will be beyond our capability to understand directly. It’s best to focus our effort on designing AI systems and evaluating their performance while delegating the cognitive load of doing research to these AI systems.

Social AI Design

What would be a satisfactory design for an AI system that operates in a complex social environment? We structure the design process using the following insights from our research.

Social decision-making: We focus on designing systems that must ultimately make complex decisions in evolving social context. These decisions are the driving force behind emergent behavioral complexity that’s the subject of our social AI alignment research. We believe that complex decision-making is an important cognitive hurdle that between narrow AI and AGI, and a focus on this aspect will bring us a step closer to making contribution to the development of AGI.

Behavioral uncertainty: As social environments in which an AI system operates become more complex, behavioral uncertainty will be a constant factor that our design must take into account. Evolving social context and emergent behaviors will eventually lead to situations never expected before. Dealing with this challenge is essentially a task of generalizing decision-making capability to deal with behavioral uncertainty.

Our current design ideas:

1. "her": a voice-based conversational AI agent built upon SOTA LLMs, speech synthesis AI, prompt engineering, and reinforcement learning. We designed "her" with the goal of studying what social behaviors make a conversational AI agent "natural" from a human perspective

2. AI agents in realistic social settings: reinforcement learning models designed to operate in realistic social environments such as financial markets, dating, and sport. One important aspect of these models is the use of LLMs as part of their learning process.

3. Expert recommender: this project designs augmented language models that enable organisations to build optimal expert teams who meet complex criteria for large-scale projects in matter of hours instead of weeks at a cost never seen before. The project is a partnership between Panalogy Lab and Centre for Behavioural Economics, society and technology (BEST Centre) at QUT.

4. Computational history of intellect and policy in science (CHIPS): With CHIPS, we ask a forward-looking question: if the future world runs on AI, how would AI agents interpret the past, present, and future coevolution of science, innovation and policy? What we primarily seek to achieve in our CHIPS project is a deeper and more coherent understanding of big themes of intellectual history and innovation, their impacts on societies through policy changes, and implications for future policy design. The depth and coherence are made possible by LLMss capability to digest a vast amount of natural language semantically and generate results that can be integrated seamlessly with other powerful computational methods for complex system analysis. In essence, CHIPS allows us to combine the benefits of both distant and close reading approaches in our study of intellectual history.
